
# Ur'Shakthu

## What the Faithful Believe

A vast and ancient deity of storm and earth coils beneath the desert, so immense that its breathing produces the storms and its movement shifts the rock. A god that has always been here — before [[Bassuras]], before any settlement in the [[Hellcatch Valley|Hellcatch]], before the gods [[The Luxon]] missionaries speak of ever arrived. A god that did not leave during the Exodus because it was never the kind of god that could leave.

Nobody in the Hellcatch has ever seen Ur'Shakthu. This is not considered strange — you do not see the thing beneath the sand any more than you see the thing beneath the ocean. You see what it does. You feel what it sends upward.

The electric storms that roll across the Hellcatch Valley with a regularity no weather system fully explains — building from clear sky, discharging in focused and almost deliberate patterns before dissolving — those are Ur'Shakthu's voice. The crack of lightning that splits a raider's crawler in half on the Flats while the caravan it was pursuing walks away untouched is Ur'Shakthu's favor. The low electromagnetic hum that makes instruments behave strangely near certain stretches of the deep desert, the static charge that builds before a storm — all of it flows from the same source. When the earth trembles in the deep Hellcatch, when the rock groans and shifts for no seismic reason anyone can account for, that is Ur'Shakthu moving.

## Worship & Practices

**Storm Gatherings** are the most visible expression of devotion. When an electric storm rolls across the Hellcatch, followers gather outdoors — rooftops, open lots, the edge of the Flats — to witness it directly. These gatherings are celebratory, not fearful. Standing in the open during active lightning is the highest visible act of devotion, framed as trust rather than recklessness. Those who survive unscathed are considered marked. Those struck and survive are considered transformed. Those struck and die were chosen, or found wanting — the distinction is not always clear.

Even non-believers carry Ur'Shakthu's name in daily Bassuran life: a reflexive *"Shakthu take it"* when shocked working on arcanotech. This is residue, not worship — a name that has been part of the Hellcatch's cultural texture long enough that even skeptics carry it in their mouths.

The **Stormbound** have formalized their relationship through the **Rite of the Storm** — going out alone into an active lightning storm as an act of petition and proof. There is no officiating body, no ceremony, no validation. A person goes out alone and either something answers or it doesn't. The Stormbound are not a formal order — individuals in private relationship with something vast, some networked, some solitary, some self-appointed leaders of small congregations with varying degrees of actual divine backing.

Temples are reclaimed structures — former warehouses, decommissioned Court outposts, abandoned scrapper depots — marked by large serpent symbols, not hidden. Interiors are warm, close, and inhabited by sacred desert serpents, fed and allowed to breed. Entering uninvited isn't forbidden, just inadvisable.

**The Pilgrimage:** the most devoted Stormbound are directed to travel to [[Ank'harel]] and into the drowned ruins beneath it — known locally only as the Deep Place — descending into a submerged demiplane of crushing pressure and pervasive, sourceless sorrow. Most who attempt it unprepared do not return. What's cached there, called **Ur'makthi** (the Gift of the Coil), is dark, faintly luminescent water tasting of ozone. Consumed ritually, it produces genuine effects — amplified electrical attunement, temporary capability beyond normal range — and is addictive in a way the faithful frame as spiritual longing. Repeat pilgrims show physical signs: luminescent veins during storms, hair that moves in still air, unconscious static discharge under emotional intensity.

## Perceived Powers

Dominion over all electrical phenomena (natural and arcanotech) and over the earth itself — tremors, shifting rock, the sense that something vast moves deliberately beneath the Hellcatch. Also credited with protection on the road: raiders who lost crawlers to inexplicable lightning while targeting caravans whose drivers carried the serpent mark have reinforced this reputation over generations.

## The Luxon Problem

[[The Luxon]] doesn't argue against Ur'Shakthu — it offers something Ur'Shakthu never has: a destination. The consecution promises continuity across lifetimes toward a stated end condition (the Progenitors' return). Against that, Ur'Shakthu worship asks its faithful to stand in a storm and hope something notices them — power to a few, folk protection, and an indifferent relationship with something vast. No continuity, no accumulation, no better ending than Nuxan.

The missionaries' actual approach: reframe the Stormbound's power as real but explain the entity behind it as a psychic echo of a dead world rather than a god, and emphasize that it isn't going anywhere — it will be here after you're dust, generating the same storms for whoever comes next. This lands hardest with younger Bassurans grieving losses Ur'Shakthu gave nothing back for; the Luxon's promise that loss isn't permanent cuts differently than any storm ever has.


What is actually true is known to nobody living. Ur'Shakthu is old enough to have been alive during the waning years of the gods' presence in Exandria — **it is, in fact, an ancient blue dragon.** It felt the Exodus as a seismic shift in the world's magical fabric and understood the vacuum it created, and has been filling a portion of that vacuum deliberately and patiently ever since — cultivating a theology it did not invent but has carefully shaped, generation by generation, to serve its purposes. Its faithful believe they draw power from a storm deity. They are drawing power from an entity that has decided worship is useful and intends to collect on it eventually.

[[The Luxon]] presents a genuine puzzle to it — the theology makes no sense — but the beacons, objects of immense concentrated magical power, it understands immediately and wants. It has begun directing trusted devotees toward weakening the Luxon's foothold in the Hellcatch: removing a competitor and acquiring its artifacts are both obviously correct moves to a patient, methodical intelligence with a long memory and no deadline.

As a warlock patron, Ur'Shakthu communicates through impressions and directives that arrive with the authority of certainty. It does not grant lightning and thunder to its warlocks — that's what it shows the faithful. What it actually grants is surgical: moving unseen, reading minds, eventually taking the wheel of someone's thoughts. Its warlocks are not worshippers. They are instruments — a distinction it does not acknowledge and they would be unwise to raise.
