# Ashtu

## What the Faithful Believe

Water in the [[Hellcatch Valley|Hellcatch]] comes from below. It always has. The wells of [[Bassuras]] go deep, deeper than they should need to, boring toward something that gives irregularly and without warning and cannot be reasoned with. Sometimes the water comes. Sometimes it doesn't. Sometimes what comes up isn't water.

Ashtu is what lives at the bottom. The snarl beneath the Flats and the groundwater beneath Bassuras are not separate phenomena — they are the same phenomenon. Pressure building in deep places, finding faults, bursting through in whatever form the crack allows: water through one fissure, wild magic through another. The distinction is geological, not theological. Ashtu does not differentiate between them.

This is the theology in its entirety. There is no elaboration, no doctrine. Ashtu is the chaos underneath everything — the water that saved the caravan, the flash flood that destroyed it, the wild magic surge that collapsed the dig site, the spring found in the deep desert. All from the same source. All equally ungovernable.

The Scar of the Desert is what practitioners call the snarl when being precise — not a wound the world is healing from, but one that is festering and growing gangrenous. It does not care about the calendar. It bleeds on its own schedule.

## Worship & Practices

Ashtu has no formal worship — what it has is a vast, largely unconscious folk practice built around the fact that you need water and Ashtu decides whether you get it. Offerings (a coin, a scrap of cloth, a broken tool) accumulate around every well and cistern in Bassuras, left on the lip or tied to the rope. Nobody organizes this. It simply happens, normalized enough that people perform it without registering it as religious.

Before drinking from an unknown water source — a puddle after rain, a spring of uncertain origin — there is a specific invocation, not a prayer but an acknowledgment: that this might kill you, that Ashtu is the reason why, and also possibly the reason it won't. The invocation varies by neighborhood; the gesture of touching the water before drinking is consistent.

There is no organized clergy. Practitioners who work specifically with Ashtu are called **Snarltouched** (less flattering) or **Deepreaders** (more serious). They do not cultivate a relationship with Ashtu — they push against the chaos and occasionally the chaos pushes back in useful directions. Their connection is less devotion and more a prolonged argument with something that has never once acknowledged the argument is happening. Wild magic is not predictable even in their hands; things happen around them with no clean explanation, taken as evidence of genuine connection rather than incompetence — a distinction that matters more to them than to anyone standing nearby when something goes wrong.

## Perceived Powers

Credited with everything that comes from below without warning: the well that ran dry (withholding), the spring found unexpectedly (giving, unexplained), the snarl's surges, wild magic flares, instrument failures, cave-ins with no seismic cause. Also patron of the genuinely lost — not geographically, but situationally: when there's no rational solution, no leverage point, no path that makes sense, that's Ashtu's domain. The deity of last resorts. When the line between cursing a god and praying to one stops mattering, that's Ashtu most present — which is as close to worship as Ashtu gets.

## The Luxon Problem

The easiest folk belief for [[The Luxon]] to argue against, and the hardest to displace. Easiest because Ashtu makes no coherent theological claims — no doctrine to refute, no clergy to discredit, no community to fracture. Missionaries can point at a well with offerings and offer natural explanations for why the water is or isn't there. They argue Ashtu is the name desperate people gave to their own helplessness, and offer agency, accumulation, and a promise that suffering has a direction instead.

Hardest because the people leaving offerings already know Ashtu isn't listening — that's not a revelation to them. They're doing the only thing available in the face of something vast, indifferent, and necessary for survival. The Luxon's promise of order and a destination assumes people want the chaos resolved. Some Bassurans have made a different peace, and the Luxon's offer reads to them not as salvation but as another thing that will eventually fail to deliver. Ashtu does not promise. It cannot fail to deliver. It simply is.
