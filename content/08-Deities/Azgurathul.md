
# Azgurathul

## What the Faithful Believe

A deity of fire and making, present in every forge flame and every spark struck from flint against steel. Older than the city. Something that could, if it chose, come through completely — level every structure in the [[Hellcatch Valley|Hellcatch]], scour the desert clean — and hasn't. The faithful don't claim it refrains out of mercy. They claim its attention is pointed at something vast and distant that makes [[Bassuras]] look like a spark in a sandstorm, and that what bleeds through the gap is incidental to whatever it actually is and whatever it's actually doing.

This is the theology. It is not comforting. The faithful do not find it comforting. They find it accurate.

Only those who've sought the connection — the Forgekissed — use the name Azgurathul. Everyone else calls it the fire, the eye, the thing in the forge. The name itself is considered significant; not said casually, because saying it is an act of acknowledgment, and acknowledgment cuts both ways. Most Bassurans have no idea there's a name at all. They just know not to look directly at a forge fire that burns wrong.

## Worship & Practices

The most embedded of the Hellcatch's folk beliefs in daily life, because fire is everywhere, Wrenches are everywhere, and Bassuras's culture of craft has been quietly devotional for as long as anyone remembers without necessarily calling itself that.

Every forge in Bassuras has the symbol above it — protective the way an evil-eye amulet is protective, not because it draws favor but because not having it is bad luck, and bad luck in a forge is how people die. The symbol's ubiquity has long outpaced the faith behind it; some were placed by believers, some because the previous owner had one, some because removing it felt like a mistake nobody wanted to own.

Before lighting a forge or beginning significant work, a Wrench invokes **The Maker's Hunger** — not a prayer for success but an acknowledgment that the fire belongs to something that could take their hands, their workshop, their life, without pausing. The same instinct as announcing yourself before entering a dark room where something large might be sleeping.

When a forge fire burns wrong — too bright, too hot, the color shifting toward something with no business coming from burning coal — practitioners call it **The Burning Eye Opening**. The correct response: finish the work quickly and efficiently, don't draw attention to yourself, don't look directly at it, don't comment on it afterward. Something vast briefly directed a fraction of its attention here; the correct response to being noticed by something that size is to be as unremarkable as possible until it looks away.

Offerings are always burned completely — nothing left intact. An offering that doesn't burn fully is a bad omen serious enough that most practitioners won't name what they think it means.

The **Forgekissed** have pushed their relationship further than daily acknowledgment and discovered something answered. Identifiable by burn scars that healed wrong, fire that behaves differently around them, an occasional wrongness in their eyes when light catches at certain angles. They don't talk about what the contact felt like. Those who've tried return to the same word, across neighborhoods and generations: *large*. Not hot. Not powerful. Just incomprehensibly, wrongly, impossibly large.

## Perceived Powers

Dominion over fire in all forms — forge fire, desert heat, arcanotech burning past tolerance, spontaneous combustion with no clear cause. Also credited with craft itself — not a Wrench's skill, but the moment a piece of work becomes more than its components. Practitioners who invoke The Maker's Hunger before significant work report the work goes better with uncomfortable consistency. Whether Azgurathul is responsible or the ritual itself produces the focus isn't a question the Forgekissed find interesting — the result is the same either way.

## The Luxon Problem

[[The Luxon]] missionaries have no framework for this one. Their standard approach requires a folk belief making refutable claims; Azgurathul worship makes almost none. It doesn't promise protection, success, continuity, community, accumulation, or transcendence. It says: something is in the fire, it is larger than you can comprehend, acknowledge it before you work, burn your offerings completely, do not look directly at it when it opens its eye.

They can argue against that theologically. They cannot argue against forge fires that burn wrong, or against the Forgekissed — whose scars healed wrong, whose eyes catch light incorrectly, who draw real power the Luxon's framework doesn't account for. They call it diabolism, planar corruption, the residue of a broken world bleeding through cracks left by catastrophe. In a city of Wrenches who've worked next to the symbol their whole lives, none of these arguments land.
