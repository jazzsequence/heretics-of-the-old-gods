# Deshretu

## What the Faithful Believe

Deshretu is not worshipped so much as acknowledged — the oldest folk belief in the [[Hellcatch Valley]], predating [[Bassuras]], predating the [[The Court of Dust|Court of Dust]], predating any living practitioner's memory. No origin story, because the desert has none; it was here before the gods arrived and remained after they left. The Exodus only confirmed what the faithful already knew: the desert outlasts everything.

Deshretu is nonbinary — a theological position reached through observation, not doctrine. The desert strips the flesh from your bones with complete indifference to what you were. It does not punish. It does not reward. It processes. A child lost to a sandstorm wasn't taken because Deshretu wanted the child; the desert simply continued being the desert. That the child is gone is not a statement about the child. It's a statement about the desert's nature, which has never changed and never will.

The central tension of Deshretu worship: you do not pray for mercy, because mercy implies Deshretu could choose otherwise. You pray for clarity — to understand your own smallness well enough to navigate it.

## Worship & Practices

Deshretu has no temples. The desert is the temple — a permanent structure built in Deshretu's honor would be a theological contradiction, since nothing permanent survives out there.

Worship is practiced through deliberate exposure and survivalism. Enduring what Deshretu offers without dying isn't proof of favor — it's proof you were adequate to the moment. The distinction matters enormously to serious practitioners.

**The Long Night** is the most sacred observance, practiced on the longest night of the year: devout believers and clerics enter the desert alone at sundown and don't return until the following sundown — 24 hours of exposure, no arcane technology permitted. Clothing, hand-crafted tools, and physical skill are allowed; everything else is stripped away, as Deshretu strips everything away eventually. Those who return are *given back* — not saved or blessed, but returned as a statement of judgment: faith renewed, slate wiped clean, body purified by having been tested and found sufficient. Those who don't return aren't mourned traditionally. They were found wanting. The desert kept what was owed.

Smaller daily devotions: letting the first cutting winds of a sandstorm pierce exposed skin before sheltering; standing uncovered facing the midday sun briefly before important decisions or after surviving something that should have killed you; sleeping outside the city walls on significant personal dates. Scrappers and runners often carry personal Deshretu rituals — a gesture before entering the Flats, a way of acknowledging the horizon before a run.

No formal clergy. Practitioners who've accumulated enough survival to be considered wise are called **Weathered** — descriptive, not a title anyone assigns. The most devoted, who draw power directly from Deshretu, are **Sandspeakers**, though they'd resist the implication that Deshretu speaks to them. The desert doesn't speak. They've simply learned to listen to silence well enough to extract meaning from it.

## Perceived Powers

Dominion over anything the desert does or can do: sandstorms, dehydration, exposure, sun-madness. The wild magic flares destabilizing the Flats near the snarl are sometimes attributed to a deeper Deshretu nature — whether Deshretu and the underground are the same entity or separate is genuinely contested among practitioners, with no authoritative answer, because there is no authority.

Also credited with the peculiar mercy of the quick death — caught in a storm, dropped by heat before suffering long, said to have been *taken cleanly*. Deshretu doesn't torture. It simply takes. A long, slow death by thirst isn't cruelty — it's Deshretu being the desert. Cold comfort, but comfort.

## The Luxon Problem

Particularly frustrating for missionaries to argue against, because Deshretu worship makes no claims they can easily refute — no afterlife promise, no claimed intervention in human affairs, nothing a missionary could call unreasonable. The faithful aren't naive; they know the desert isn't a person. The standard [[The Luxon]] argument (folk gods as primitive explanation superseded by the Luxon's light) lands badly here, since Deshretu practitioners were never claiming explanation — they were claiming relationship. The desert is real. Their relationship to it is real. What is the Luxon improving on?

More aggressive missionaries frame it as a death cult — the Long Night is an easy target — arguing the faith asks followers to accept suffering and death as inevitable rather than seeking transcendence. This lands better with younger Bassurans tired of being told endurance is the highest virtue, a tension the Luxon is well aware is useful to them.
